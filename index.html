What Is Crude Oil?

Formation, classification & why it matters

Crude Oil — The Basics

Crude oil is a naturally occurring fossil fuel formed over millions of years from ancient marine organisms buried under sedimentary rock.

It is the single most traded commodity on earth and the backbone of the global energy system.

Key fact: Oil provides ~31% of global primary energy and is the feedstock for thousands of everyday products — from gasoline to plastics to pharmaceuticals.

API Gravity & Crude Classification

API Gravity is the industry-standard density measurement. Higher number = lighter oil = easier to refine = higher price.

ClassificationAPI GravityCharacteristicsExample
Light> 31°Flows easily, high gasoline yieldWTI (39.6°)
Medium22° – 31°Moderate processing neededArab Light (32°)
Heavy< 22°Thick, requires upgrading/diluentWCS (20.5°)
Extra-Heavy< 10°Bitumen-like, needs upgradersOrinoco (5-15°)
Trading rule: Light sweet crude always commands the highest price. The lighter and sweeter, the more valuable.

Sweet vs Sour Crude

Sweet Crude (<0.5% sulfur)

  • Easier and cheaper to refine
  • Higher yield of gasoline & diesel
  • Commands a premium price
  • Examples: WTI (0.24%), Brent (0.37%)

Sour Crude (>0.5% sulfur)

  • Requires hydrotreating / hydrodesulfurization
  • More expensive to process
  • Trades at a discount
  • Examples: Mars (1.9%), Arab Heavy (2.8%)
Key distinction: API Gravity measures density (light/heavy). Sulfur content measures sweetness (sweet/sour). Both affect price and which refineries can process it.

The Oil Value Chain

Upstream → Midstream → Downstream

Oil Value Chain Overview

Highest oil price sensitivity

→

Midstream

Transport, Store & Process

Pipelines. Terminals. Processing plants.

Revenue = Fee-based / Take-or-Pay

Lowest oil price sensitivity

→

Downstream

Refine crude into gasoline, diesel, jet fuel.

Revenue = Crack Spread

Critical concept: Refiners' margins can actually IMPROVE when oil prices fall — cheap input, sticky product prices.

Upstream — Exploration & Production

How E&Ps Make Money

  • Revenue = production volume × realized oil/gas price
  • Key metric: FCF Yield (Free Cash Flow / Market Cap)
  • Post-2020 era: Capital discipline — return cash, don't drill recklessly
  • Watch breakeven prices: US shale avg $45-65/bbl
The shift: Pre-2020 E&Ps prioritized growth. Post-2020 they prioritize shareholder returns — buybacks + dividends.

Key Concepts

Decline Rates

Shale wells decline 50-70% in Year 1. Companies must constantly drill to maintain production — the "treadmill effect."

Permian Basin

The crown jewel of US shale — West Texas & SE New Mexico. Lowest breakeven costs, highest returns. Home to FANG, EOG, DVN.

Midstream — Pipelines, Storage & Processing

The "Toll Booth" Business

  • Revenue from fee-based contracts, not commodity prices
  • Take-or-pay contracts: shipper pays regardless of usage
  • Many structured as MLPs — high distribution yields, K-1 tax forms
  • NGL processing adds value — ethane, propane, butane
Why investors love midstream: Stable cash flows, high yields (5-8%), low commodity sensitivity. The "bond proxy" of energy.

Infrastructure Assets

Pipelines: 190,000+ miles of oil pipelines in the US alone. Massive capex to build, near-zero marginal cost once operating.

Storage: Cushing, OK is the delivery point for WTI futures. Tank levels are closely watched by traders.

LNG Terminals: Liquefy gas at -260°F for export. US LNG exports have surged since 2016.

Downstream — Refining & Marketing

How Refiners Make Money

  • Profit = Crack spread (product price − crude input cost)
  • The 3-2-1 crack spread: 2 bbls gasoline + 1 bbl distillate from 3 bbls crude
  • Inverse sensitivity: cheap crude + high product demand = fat margins
  • Turnarounds (scheduled maintenance) temporarily cut throughput

Refinery Complexity

Nelson Complexity Index measures a refinery's ability to process different crude types.

Higher complexity = structural margin advantage

  • FCC — Cracks heavy gas oil into gasoline
  • Hydrocracking — Produces cleaner diesel & jet fuel
  • Coking — Converts bottom-of-the-barrel residual

Complex refineries can run cheap heavy/sour crude and still produce premium products.

Global Oil Benchmarks

Three benchmark crudes price the majority of the world's oil:

WTI

West Texas Intermediate

  • Traded on NYMEX (CME Group)
  • Delivery: Cushing, Oklahoma
  • 39.6° API, 0.24% sulfur
  • US domestic benchmark

Brent

North Sea Blend

  • Traded on ICE London
  • Prices ~75% of world oil
  • 38° API, 0.37% sulfur
  • THE global benchmark

Dubai/Oman

Middle East Benchmark

  • Traded on DME
  • Prices Asia-Pacific crude
  • 31° API, 2.0% sulfur
  • Medium sour grade
Brent-WTI spread reflects US vs global supply dynamics. When US oversupplied → WTI discount widens.

E&P — The Capital Discipline Era

Pre-2020: Growth at All Costs

  • Drill, drill, drill — production growth was king
  • Outspend cash flow, fund with debt & equity
  • Investors destroyed billions in value
  • Shale boom led to oversupply → price crashes

Post-2020: Capital Discipline

  • Cap reinvestment at 50-60% of cash flow
  • Return the rest via buybacks + dividends
  • Variable dividend models (DVN pioneered)
  • Maintenance-mode production, not growth
Result: E&P stocks have massively outperformed since 2021 as free cash flow surged and capital allocation improved.

The 3-2-1 Crack Spread

For every 3 barrels of crude input, a refinery produces ~2 barrels of gasoline and ~1 barrel of distillate. The spread between output value and input cost is the refiner's gross margin.

Nelson Complexity

Complex refineries (NCI > 12) can process cheap heavy/sour crude and still produce premium products. This is a structural, hard-to-replicate competitive advantage.

Oil Pricing & Futures

Contango, backwardation & the data that moves markets

Contango vs Backwardation

The shape of the futures curve reveals the market's view of supply/demand balance:

Contango (Oversupply)

Far-month futures > near-month (spot)

  • Market has excess supply or weak near-term demand
  • Incentivizes storage plays — buy cheap spot, sell expensive futures
  • Classic sign: oil tankers used as floating storage
  • Example: COVID-19 crash (April 2020) — extreme contango

Backwardation (Tight Supply)

Near-month (spot) > far-month futures

  • Market is tight — oil is more valuable today than tomorrow
  • Incentivizes drawing down inventories
  • Bullish signal for producers — immediate cash flows higher
  • Example: 2022 post-Ukraine invasion — steep backwardation
Trading insight: Persistent backwardation is the strongest bullish signal in oil markets. It means physical barrels are in high demand NOW.

Geopolitics & the 2026 Crisis

OPEC+, chokepoints & the Strait of Hormuz

OPEC+ — The Supply Cartel

What Is OPEC+?

  • 13 OPEC members + 10 allies (led by Russia)
  • Controls ~40% of global oil supply
  • Sets production quotas at periodic meetings
  • Goal: manage prices by controlling supply

Key Members

Saudi Arabia (de facto leader), UAE, Iraq, Kuwait, Russia, Kazakhstan, Nigeria, Iran, Venezuela, Libya

Why OPEC+ Matters for Traders

  • Production cuts → reduced supply → higher prices
  • Quota increases → more supply → lower prices
  • Compliance monitoring — do members cheat?
  • Meeting outcomes can swing oil 5-10% in a day
2024-26 theme: OPEC+ has maintained significant production cuts (~5.8 mb/d withheld) to support prices above $80/bbl.

Critical Oil Chokepoints

Narrow waterways through which massive volumes of oil must transit. Disruption = instant price spike.

Venezuela

The sleeping giant of oil reserves

Venezuela — World's Largest Reserves

304B
barrels proven reserves (#1 globally)
~900K
b/d current production (from 3.5 mb/d peak)
The paradox: The country with the world's largest reserves produces less than 1 mb/d due to sanctions, mismanagement, and infrastructure collapse.

Key Facts

Orinoco Belt: Vast extra-heavy oil region. 75%+ of reserves. API gravity 5-15°, sulfur 4-6%. Requires upgraders or diluent to flow.

PDVSA: State-owned oil company. Decimated by political appointments, brain drain, and chronic underinvestment.

OFAC Sanctions: US sanctions limit who can do business with Venezuela. Chevron holds the only active US license.

Venezuela — The Reopening Play

Why It Matters Now

  • US policy shifting toward engagement (sanctions relief)
  • Global need for heavy crude supply (Canadian alternatives constrained)
  • Potential to add 500K-1M+ b/d over 3-5 years with investment
  • WCS discount could narrow if Venezuela competes for refinery throughput

The Chevron Advantage

CVX is the clear first-mover in Venezuela:

  • Only US major with active OFAC license
  • Never fully exited — maintained JV relationships
  • Already ramping production in existing fields
  • Gulf Coast refineries configured for Venezuelan heavy crude
Thesis: Venezuela represents the largest untapped oil optionality in the world. CVX owns the pole position.

Investment Thesis

Putting it all together

Bull vs Bear — Oil Macro Outlook

Bull Case ($100+ Brent)

  • Hormuz crisis escalation / prolonged closure
  • OPEC+ maintains deep cuts, demand resilient
  • SPR at historic lows — limited buffer
  • China stimulus drives demand recovery
  • Chronic underinvestment limits non-OPEC supply growth

Bear Case ($55-65 Brent)

  • Hormuz fully reopens, geopolitical risk premium fades
  • OPEC+ compliance breaks down, supply floods market
  • Global recession crushes demand
  • EV adoption accelerates, peak demand narrative strengthens
  • US shale growth surprises to the upside
